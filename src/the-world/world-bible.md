*When the end of the world came, it was neither quick nor remotely merciful. The world took decades to die, and worse still, God was a no-show on the apocalypse's VIP guest list. But we are not telling a story about the end of the world. This is a story about what happens once the end has come, gone, and the world still keeps on turning.*
## The Premise

**TL;DR:** *It’s **200 TE** (roughly the mid-23rd century CE). The old world died slowly and painfully, and no god arrived to stop it. Humanity survives in fortified city-states called **Basilicas**, protected by magic and surrounded by monster-infested wilds. Nightfolk live hidden: and, since the Masquerade fell, openly registered: among them. This isn’t the story of the apocalypse; It’s the story of the people living in the half drowned world it left behind.*

The end of the world was neither quick nor merciful: it took **decades** to die, and then the world kept turning anyway. Humanity no longer dominates the planet. It shares the ruins with **aberrations** and resurrected prehistoric predators.

Survival now rests on the old reliables: **strength in numbers, tall walls, and stubborn will.** The nations of the old world are gone. In their place stand the **Basilicas**: fortified city-state metropolises, the only places humanity can truly live and grow. Between one Basilica and the next, the land is wild and deadly. People born outside the walls will sell *everything*: down to their souls: for a chance to live inside them.

**Population:** No more than **500 million** worldwide: A mere **5%** of humanity’s ancient peak.

## State of the World

The world in the 23rd century is so changed as to be unrecognizable. A full two-thirds of the land has sunk, consuming Europe, Australia, Japan, and much of the Americas below shallow seas—Any city that was below 700m above sea level pre sundering is lost to the tides. The land left is largely uninhabited, filled with aberrants, various forms of radiation hazards, and strange anomalous phenomena more often lethal than helpful—such as the universally feared wyrdstorms that twist reality and birth aberrants.

The only safe places from such hazards are places behind a hearthstone’s defensive barriers, as they are able to repel the worst of the Aberrants and deflect Wyrdstorms. Such settlements are rare, countable in the few hundreds globally, and all are anchored to one of the Basilicas, the city-state metropolises that hold more people between them than all the safe small cities and villages combined. Of course, these barriers are not absolute in the face of a powerful and dogged enemy—The fate of Mount Hagen’s people taught the world that all too well.

### **The Basilicas Today**

*What follows is common knowledge — the reputation each Basilica carries in the mouths of traders, travelers, and tavern gossips across the League. Your character has probably heard most of this, whether or not any of it is fair.*

#### *Addis Ababa — “The Granary”*

The Ethiopian highlands became the best real estate on the planet when the waters rose: vast, fertile, temperate, and high above the tide. Addis Ababa feeds a hungry world and knows exactly what that is worth. Its Highland Granaries are strategic infrastructure on par with any Hearthstone, its coffee monopoly is enforced with a straight face and real warships, and its reputation for scrupulous neutrality makes it the League's favorite venue for arbitration — more treaties have been signed over its coffee ceremonies than in any council chamber. Cross Addis and no one will attack you; you will simply, slowly, stop being invited to eat.

#### *Antananarivo — “The Far Island”*

The most isolated Basilica, and the strangest. Madagascar's biosphere was already like nowhere else on Earth; two centuries of wyrdstorms made it something adjacent to a fever dream, and the highland city rules an island of towering baobab-things and singing megafauna. Its alchemists are without peer — reagents that exist nowhere else in the world grow within a week's (terrifying) trek of the walls — and its exports are bought sight-unseen at any price. Visitors find the locals warm, unhurried, and unsettlingly casual about the impossible. Getting there is the hard part; some years, the storm-belts allow no ships through at all, and Antananarivo simply doesn't mind.

#### *Bengaluru — “The Glyphcode City”*

The old tech city became the new tech city; only the substrate changed. Bengaluru's engineering guilds wrote the Glyphcode Standards — the shared notation that lets a paratech device etched in Bogotá be repaired in Chino — and that quiet act of standardization made it indispensable to every Basilica on the map. The rising seas turned the plateau's edge into dramatic cliff-coastline, and its harbor-lifts lower cargo hundreds of meters from city to sea in great counterweighted cages. Fast-talking, meritocratic, and perpetually mid-argument: Bengaluru's guildhalls treat a design flaw the way other cities treat a blood feud.

#### *Bogotá — “The Emerald Vault"*

New Tenochtitlan's great rival on the Andean spine, and it stings Bogotá deeply that the rivalry is not considered equal. Its Hearthstone is seated in the old Salt Cathedral, a consecrated underground labyrinth that doubles as the most secure vault in the League — Basilica elites and magnates alike store what they cannot afford to lose here. Above ground, guilds of goldsmith-artificers carry an unbroken pre-Columbian craft tradition into paratech, and the emerald-and-leystone exchange sets prices half the world trades by. Polished, proud, and patient: Bogotá plays the long game, and usually wins it.

#### *Calgary — “Land of the Beast Lords”*

The prairie survived the drowning of the world by simple virtue of altitude, and the people who resettled it kept the old ranching soul — they just changed what they ranch. Where every other Basilica answers the aberrant question with walls and harpoons, Calgary answers it with a rope, inhuman patience, and just a bit of magic: its beast-tamers have spent three generations proving that some of the Wyrd's monsters can be gentled. Their city is filled with tamed aberrant beasts; most famously its herds of six-legged giant fire-breathing bison known as ‘Emberbreaths’ formally, ‘Smoke Puffers’ locally, ‘Prairie drakes’ in fanciful traveler’s tales. Most people think them a myth—until they stand at the barrier's edge and watch living proof of Calgarian wyrdtaming prowess grazing placidly under its shimmer.

The Calgarian wyrdtaming techniques are jealously guarded, and the annual Stampede: part festival, part public trial of nerve where tamers break new stock before a roaring crowd — draws travelers from three continents. The rest of the League regard the whole enterprise as magnificent lunacy, right up until they place their orders for draft-beasts that can haul a caravan through terrain that eats trucks (and people) with impunity. Friendly, unpretentious, and possessed of the particular calm of people who work daily with things that could eat them: Calgarians will shake anyone's hand, Nightfolk included with utter sincerity. To them, the only question that matters is your work ethic… *And if you're good with animals*.

#### *Chino — “The Shrine on the Shore"*

When the seas took Japan, they left an archipelago of alpine peaks, and Chino sits at its new waterline. The city grew around the ancient Lake Suwa shrine complex, and its paratech is famously wound through with shrine-craft: wards written as prayer-strips, Hearthstone conduits blessed as often as they are etched. Its wealth comes from the salvage fleets — hard-eyed divers who descend into the drowned sprawl of old Tokyo and Nagoya to bring up Old World relics, and who do not talk about what else lives down there. Reserved, ritualistic, and quietly one of the most Nightfolk-tolerant Basilicas: the line between a registered BRD and a local kami has always been politely blurry here.

#### *City-isle of Purgatory — “City of Exiles and Mysteries”*

The only true settlement on the Wandering Isle, and the strangest member of the League by a comfortable margin. Purgatory began as a dumping ground — the polite oubliette where the Basilicas sent the too-powerful-to-kill, back when the Governor's Toll made residency a life sentence — and its founding stock of exiles, fugitives, and inconvenient people never quite washed out of its character. The waygates changed everything a decade ago: now Purgatory is a freeport where every Basilica's goods, agents, and secrets pass through the same crowded streets, governed day-to-day by the Council of Six and its five Grand Factions under the distant eyes of the Triune. It is the most openly supernatural city on Earth, the easiest place in the world to disappear, and the worst place in the world to assume nobody important is watching. It’s said everyone in Purgatory came from somewhere else, everyone has an angle, nothing is how it seems, and nothing is free—ESPECIALLY things that claim to be.

#### *New Tenochtitlan — “The First Hearth"*

The birthplace of the Hearthbind Ritual and the oldest Basilica, built proudly atop old Mexico City, and it never lets anyone forget either fact. Its Sorcery Academies are the finest in the world, its sorcerer clans are old money and older grudges, and its politics are a blood sport conducted in perfect courtesy. Beyond the barrier, anomalous jungle has swallowed the valley, and things that should have stayed extinct hunt in it — dinosaur-hunting is both a rite of passage and a lucrative industry. If you want to learn magic, you come here. If you want to survive learning magic, you learn the politics first.

#### *Kathmandu — “The City of Ten Thousand Shrines"*

The Himalaya shrugged off the drowning of the world, and Kathmandu endures in its shadow much as it always has — older than the catastrophe, and quietly certain it will outlast the next one. It is the spiritual capital of the Third Era: pilgrims of every faith and species climb to it, and regard it as something close to holy ground, for the veil between flesh and spirit is thin along the high passes. Disputes between the material and the immaterial get settled here, by mediators who take tea with ghosts. Serene on the surface; underneath, a bazaar of secrets, relics, and favors owed to things without bodies.

#### *Johannesburg — “The Deep City”*

Johannesburg was built on the deepest mines humanity ever dug, and the Wyrd found them before the miners came back. Its shafts now yield the richest green leystone seams known, feeding foundries that make it the heavy-industrial heart of the League — if a Basilica runs a machine bigger than a wagon, odds are Johannesburg’s factories built it. There is only one exception—New Colombia’s stranglehold on paratech weapon research and production is considered peerless to Johannesburg’s endless annoyance. It is whispered that in the mines the deep levels are cordoned, the miners' unions keep their own exorcists on payroll, and everyone in the Gold Reef knows the rule: you do not follow lights, voices, or old friends below the safety line.

#### *La Paz–El Alto — “The Roof of the World's Ruin"*

The highest Basilica on Earth, terraced into a canyon rim so far above the new seas that the drowning of the world is, locally, a rumor. Its districts are stitched together by paratech gondola lines that never stop running, and its thin-air foundries produce alloys no lowland forge can match. The famous Witches' Market operates in the open here and always has — La Paz never really enforced either Masquerade, and its sorcerer-vendors will sell you a blessing, a curse, or an honest appraisal of which one you actually need. Insular, vertiginous, and superstitious in the way of people who turned out to be right.

#### *Mount Hagen — “The Fallen Bastion”*

Seated in the Melanesian highlands, Mount Hagen was once one of the Great Bastions. Besieged by the Hag-Queen Rangda in 130 TE (70 years ago) and abandoned by its peers, it fell after three years. It is spoken of rarely, and traveled to never: behind its stolen barrier, it has become a hell on earth where humans are raised as livestock. Its fate is the reason the League exists—and the name every Basilican diplomat reaches for when an ally hesitates.

#### *New Colombia — “The Star Spangled City”*

The waters took the coasts and the bombs took the rest, but a nation, New Colombia insists, is not a place — it is a Constitution, and they have the original locked in a vault beneath the city to prove it. Built on the ruins of old Salt Lake City — high enough that the drowning of the world arrived as a swollen inland sea rather than an ending — New Colombia does not consider itself a successor to the United States; it considers itself a continuation that their origin stories claim fled all the way from Washington DC shortly before the city burned and endured a century as a caravan of patriots. It prides itself on its military, which all able bodied citizens must spend time in. The aberrant living statues that roam in the pits of the Bingham Canyon Mine some few miles beyond the city walls contain the second highest quality green Leystone available; much of it feeds their paratech weaponry manufacturing sector. Everyone—to Johannesburg’s endless annoyance—considers Colombian style aetheric firearms the gold standard. The League largely finds New Colombia’s “we are the last light of true civilization” mindset as sanctimonious as it is exhausting, and yet they remain indispensable all the same.

#### *Nairobi — “The Hunter's Gate”*

The Rift Valley below Nairobi is now an inland sea, and the things that grew in it grew *large*. Nairobi answered the way Nairobi would: it became the aberrant-hunting capital of the world. Every great hunter guild keeps a hall here, the leviathan fisheries land catches that feed the ichor refineries day and night, and half the League's refined leystone fuel passes through its ledgers. It is loud, young, dangerous, and gloriously alive — a boomtown where a nobody with a sharp harpoon can die famous. Nightfolk are welcome, in the wary way of a city full of professional monster-killers: *Prove yourself, and no sudden movements.*

#### *Lhasa — “The High Refuge”*

When China burned and then drowned, the survivors climbed, and Lhasa took them in. The Potala Palace now seats one of the great Hearthstones, and the monastic orders that tend it also tend the largest archive of salvaged Old World and occult texts in existence — scholars beg for reading access; most die waiting. It is a contemplative, tightly governed city of prayer-wheels and refugees' descendants, and it is also the subject of the League's most persistent ugly rumor: that one of the undisclosed prisons of the Awakened Censure sits somewhere in the mountains above it. Lhasa does not dignify the question—Many see the stony silence on the matter as its own sort of answer.

#### *Salamanca — “The Holy City”*

Iberia drowned, but the old golden city on the meseta kept its spires dry, and the survivors of a hundred flooded dioceses walked uphill to it. The new Vatican rose from Salamanca's ancient churches, convents, and university halls, and the city is now the beating heart of the largest surviving faith on Earth: pilgrim roads converge on it from three continents, its exorcists are requested by name across the League, and its theological archives quietly rival Lhasa's — a comparison both cities pretend not to make. In this Theocratic city-state, sorcery is highly restricted; illegal outside of the church sanctioned (and trained) *Teúrgo*, who insist their powers are holy and come from God. It is also the stronghold of the Society of Leopold, and therein lies the chill: registered Nightfolk are *technically* legal in Salamanca, as the Pax Eldritus demands; they are also watched every hour they remain and are not given a chance to explain themselves before their heads go flying. Nothing is ever said. Nothing needs to be. The Holy City forgives sinners, but it punishes their sins with extreme prejudice.

##### ⚠ Traveler's Advisory: Salamanca

***If you work magic:** Sorcery is illegal in Salamanca unless practiced by the church-sanctioned **Teúrgo**. There is no visitor's exemption, no professional courtesy, and no "it's legal where I'm from." Guild papers from Bengaluru, an Academy diploma from New Tenochtitlan, a Witches' Market stall license from La Paz — none of it means anything on the meseta. Practicing unsanctioned sorcery is prosecuted as heresy, and the trial is not the part of the process you should be worried about.*

***If you're Nightfolk:** Your BRD registration is valid in Salamanca, but in the most technical of senses. The Society of Leopold is strong here, and the local chapter of the Order of Vigiles is widely rumored to be in the church's pocket. Writs of execution in Salamanca are suspected of being issued posthumously and having all the investigation conformations of acceptable use of force rubber stamped, though the League has never managed to make an inquiry stick. If a Leopoldine takes an interest in you, the paperwork will be in order by the time anyone asks what happened to you.*

***The practical advice**, as any caravan master will give it: transact your business, keep your gifts sheathed, sleep outside the walls if you can, and be on the pilgrim road by week's end. Salamanca is genuinely safe, hospitable, and orderly — for humans who neither work wonders nor keep company with those who do.*

#### *Sana'a — “The Star-Chandlery”*

When Arabia drowned, the Yemeni highlands became an island, and Sana'a's ancient mud-brick towers — already thousands of years old — now crown it. The city rebuilt the incense trade for a new age: its groves supply the resins and ritual materials that half the world's warding-craft burns, carried by the famous Incense Fleets. But its true fame is the Star-Chandlers, the navigator-astrologer guild whose charts predict, with unsettling accuracy, where the Wandering Isle will surface next. How they do it is Sana'a's best-kept secret, and every faction on Purgatory's Council has tried to buy, steal, or marry into it. Ancient, vertical, incense-shadowed, and smug about all three.

#### *Tabriz — “The Bazaar of the Third Era”*

Everything is for sale in Tabriz, and always has been. Its Grand Bazaar was old before the Old World fell; today its covered miles are the largest marketplace on Earth, where League currency, Basilica futures, caravan shares, and quieter merchandise change hands under one roof. Its signature craft is glyph-weaving — enchantment worked into textile rather than etched in leystone — and a Tabrizi carpet that keeps a room warm, silent, or *unnoticed* costs more than a house.

#### *Ulaanbaatar — “The City That Chose to Stop”*

The steppe clans wandered through the whole Itinerant Period and were better at it than anyone — and when they finally raised a Hearthstone, they did it on their own terms. Ulaanbaatar is the caravan capital of the world: its Great Caravanserai outfits, insures, and blesses the overland trade of three continents, and its stormchaser fleets ride *toward* wyrdstorms to harvest fresh leystone from the twisted ground behind them, the most profitable insane profession in the Third Era. Half the city still lives in gers by choice and empties onto the steppe each season. Hospitality is sacred, cowardice is not, and every deal is sealed on horseback at least ceremonially.

## 5\. How The World Works Now

**TL;DR:** *Old World technologies no longer work reliably: reality is too unstable. Modern life runs on **Paratech** (complex, interlocking enchantments that mimic technology), anchored by city-core **Hearthstones** that hold back the storms and the monsters.*

### **Paratech (Paracausal Technology)**

Old World technology (“scitech”) is too fragile for the third era: It was built under the presumption of a reality with static rules and goes haywire the moment a wyrdstorm nears. **Paratech** is really the sorcerous art of enchanting, elevated into a standardized practice by *paratech engineers.* It draws on a settlement’s Heartstone energy field and uses carefully etched **leystone** crystals interlinked by pathways of silver and gold to route energy through layered enchantments in a dependable and autonomous fashion— The end result is reproducing the *effect* of lightbulbs, microwaves, and more, if not their old inner mechanisms.

### **Leystone**

Leystone is the Oil of the third era; the one resource no one ever has enough of, yet their entire civilization runs on it. Vital as both the foundational material that Paratech is built from and a fuel to power lesser Hearthstones, its acquisition is one of the key topics that drive political calculus of the era. It is extracted from two primary sources: It forms in large seams from natural rock twisted by a Wyrdstorm, and can be alchemically refined from the ichor of Aberrants. The higher its quality, the clearer it is; exceedingly high quality leystone is akin to looking through lightly glowing colored glass.

There are three known types of Leystone:

#### *Green Leystone*

Prized by paracausal engineers, it is the only known stable substance able to hold multiple active enchantments at once, making it the workhorse material for etching the complex glyph formulas that make paratech work.

#### *Blue/Purple Leystone*

Exceedingly aether dense, its innate unstable nature makes it valuable as a fuel (and a material to make bombs). Common fuelstock is blue, but rarely a purple variant shows up which contains twice the energy (and also goes for about twice the usual rate).

#### *Red Leystone*

Found only on ruby beach of the Wandering Isle, it is to locals as “Heartglass”. Red Leystone contains, pound-for-pound ten to twelve times the energy of even high quality Purple variety, making it incredibly sought after as the prized (and restricted) export of the Wandering Isle. However, unlike other types of Leystone, it is known to have a strange will of its own that seeks to merge with living things; attempts to make paratech with it have resulted in erratic creations seemingly with a mind of their own.

### **Hearthstones**

Hearthstones are the beating heart of every permanent settlement the world over. They moderate weather, power the paratech grid, and: most importantly: anchor the **defensive barriers** that deflect wyrdstorms and repel aberrant beasts. 

There are three tiers:

| Tier | Protects | Powered By |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Minor** | A village | Natural leyline flow |
| **Lesser** | A small city | Leystone-fueled generators |
| **Greater** | A metropolis (Basilica) | A **quintessence reactor**\* |

\*Greater Hearthstones require truly staggering amounts of aether: enough that they run on immense subterranean **quintessence reactors**. Everyone knows the reactors exist; almost no one outside a rarefied few knows exactly *how* they work.

## Living in the Shadows

**TL;DR:** *Nightfolk are legally allowed inside the Basilicas and their satellite settlements if they register and behave. Register as a **BRD** (Benign Reality Deviant), obey the **Pax Eldritus**, and you’re treated as a citizen. Prey on humans (or get caught acting too inhuman) and a **writ of execution** goes to the Hunters. Discretion is survival.*

**The Masquerade’s End.** The Second Masquerade lasted only \~30 years before the Shadow Directorate suddenly decided to reveal the existence of the Nightfolk living among humanity. The result was predictable: fear, riots, and lynchings: with *far more humans* dying in the “monster hunts”than actual Nightfolk: until the Basilicas cracked down.

Today, safety depends on *downplaying* the danger within the walls. So long as mankind can be convinced the real threat is *out there* in the trackless wilds: and that sorcerers and aberrant-hunters keep the walls safe: people don’t reach for torches and pitchforks over their neighbors.

**Pax Eldritus (Peace of the Eldritch).** The bargain that makes true coexistence possible:

* Nightfolk must register as **BRDs (Benign Reality Deviants).** So long as they aren’t *caught* acting hostile or predatory toward humans, they live with the full rights of any citizen.

* Accusations are investigated by the **Order of Vigiles.** A confirmed breach revokes BRD status and requires issuing a **writ of execution** to the **Aberrant Hunters**, who may then legally kill the offender.

* Protection runs both ways: **unsanctioned** hunts are also investigated by the Vigiles, and rogue hunters “tend to suddenly go missing”.

**The unspoken rule:** *Discretion is the better part of valor.* Hide obviously inhuman features and abilities in unknown company; reveal your nature only to a trusted few (and even then, spin it favorably). BRD status is fragile: even the *perception* of using your inhuman advantages over a normal human can cost it. Easiest, always, to simply seem human from the start.

**On Hunters.** Hunters are humans who devote their lives to killing **Aberrants.** Most have lost someone to one. Some curdle into militant zealots who see every Supernatural as a tragedy waiting to happen; others overcome the pain and become true guardians, working so that no one else suffers as they did.

## The Splats: An Overview

Full character creation information for each is in the [Character Creation Guide](/character-creation/).

**Vampire (Lilum).** When Caine walked into Oblivion, Lilith intervened and gave every vampire a choice. Those who accepted rebirth through her covenant became the Lilum — still vampiric in nature, but tied to the Dark Mother rather than the First Murderer. The old Camarilla, Sabbat, and Anarch exist only in historical accounts; in modern nights, Vampires organize through the Factions. The six common bloodlines are Ventrue, Toreador, Malkavian, Gangrel, Nosferatu, and True Brujah.

**Werewolf (Garou).** When Gaia the Elder died, every Garou not sheltered in a spiritual realm was erased from existence. Eshtarra's ascension as Gaia the Younger remade them as the Gaian Shifters — servants bound to cleanse the world's spiritual contamination. They carry no silver bane; their bane is obsidian. The six surviving tribes are Black Furies, Fianna, Get of Fenris, Glass Walkers, Silent Striders, and Children of Eshtarra.

**Lost.** The Great Wyrdstorm cracked the gates to Arcadia and the Dreaming open. What remains are three distinct peoples: the Lost (playable), the Dreamborne (rare, playable), and the Wyrd-born (Storyteller-controlled). The six seemings are Beasts, Darklings, Elementals, Fairest, Ogres, and Wizened.

**Hellion.** The Hungry Dead — those who have died, endured the Thousand Hells, and returned changed. Not vampires, though both are undead. Hellions walk a Dharma path and manage the balance between their Ego and their Shadow.

**Sorcerers.** Awakened magic died with the Mages. Sorcerers survived because their magic was always smaller in scale and more rigid — a "paint by numbers" practice that bureaucracy could legislate. The six schools are Artificers, Augurs, Druids, Ossians, Wayfarers, and Fury Shapers.

**Psychics.** Psychic ability can emerge in any human born inside the Basilica — and it is a liability, not a gift, since the general population mistakes it for Awakened magic. Every Psychic learns to mask their ability as Sorcerer-style practice.

**Humans.** The majority of Basilica Purgatory. They run the city, staff its institutions, and outnumber every supernatural type combined. Human characters choose a Profession. Half-Damned are humans with one foot in the supernatural world: Kinfolk, Ghouls, Projectors, and Dhampir.

**Hunters.** In Basilica Purgatory, Hunters don't hunt supernaturals to destroy them — they exist to maintain balance and keep the Veil intact.

| **Shadow Directorate** | The supernatural governing body. It declared the Second Masquerade, later revealed the supernatural world, and is now recognized as co-equal to the League Council. |
| **The Sundering** | The decades-long cataclysm that nearly destroyed reality and remade the world. |
| **Third Era, The (TE)** | The current age. Its start (0 TE / 2078 CE) is dated to the aftermath of the Sundering. Present day: **200 TE.** |
| **Wyrdstorm** | A reality-warping storm. The great one during the Sundering is remembered as the **Great Wyrdstorm.** |
